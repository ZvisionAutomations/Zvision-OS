# Adult Learning Principles

Theoretical foundations for designing courses that actually work for adult learners.

---

## Why Adults Learn Differently

Adult learners differ from children in 6 critical ways (Knowles' Andragogy):

| Dimension | Children (Pedagogy) | Adults (Andragogy) |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Self-concept | Dependent | Self-directed |
| Experience | Limited background | Rich experience to draw from |
| Readiness | Age-based | Problem/role-based |
| Orientation | Future (store for later) | Immediate application |
| Motivation | External (grades) | Internal (relevance, growth) |
| Need to know | "Because I said so" | Must know WHY before learning |

**Design implication:** Always answer "why does this matter to me right now?" before teaching anything.

---

## Knowles' Andragogy — 6 Assumptions

| Assumption | What It Means for Course Design |
|------------|--------------------------------|
| 1. Self-concept | Give learners choices: order, pace, project topic |
| 2. Experience | Build on what they already know, use their examples |
| 3. Readiness | Teach what's immediately useful in their current situation |
| 4. Orientation | Frame lessons as problems to solve, not topics to cover |
| 5. Motivation | Connect to career, income, status, or identity goals |
| 6. Need to know | Open every module with "why this matters to you" |

## Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle

Adults learn through experience, not just information.

```
Concrete Experience (Do)
        ↓
Reflective Observation (Observe)
        ↓
Abstract Conceptualization (Think)
        ↓
Active Experimentation (Plan)
        ↓ (back to top)
```

**Course design translation:**
1. **Do** — Give an exercise or case before the theory
2. **Observe** — Debrief what happened, what they noticed
3. **Think** — Introduce the framework or concept
4. **Plan** — Apply to their own context

## Self-Directed Learning

Adults want control over their learning. Design for it:

- **Choice of path** — Offer optional advanced modules
- **Control of pace** — Self-paced over live-only where possible
- **Agency in application** — Exercises use their real projects
- **Progress visibility** — They choose when to move on

**What kills self-direction:** Rigid sequences for simple content, no skip options, no context for why this order matters.

## Motivation Drivers

| Driver | How to Design For It |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Instrumental** (career, income) | Show ROI early. Student testimonials. Salary/revenue examples. |
| **Social** (belonging, status) | Community, peer recognition, cohort identity |
| **Intrinsic** (curiosity, mastery) | Challenges, stretch projects, depth options |
| **Transformational** (identity) | Frame as "becoming X" not just "learning Y" |

**Rule:** Identify which driver is dominant for your audience. Design accordingly.

## Scaffolding

Adult learners need structure to transition from novice to expert.

| Level | What They Need | Design Approach |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Novice | Explicit steps, worked examples | Step-by-step walkthroughs |
| Developing | Guided practice with feedback | Templates + coached exercises |
| Competent | Challenging scenarios | Case studies, open-ended projects |
| Expert | Autonomy | Optional advanced paths |

**Scaffolding rule:** Remove support progressively. Module 1 is highly guided. Module 5 should require independent judgment.

## Problem-Based Learning (PBL)

Present the problem first, then teach the tools to solve it.

**Structure:**
1. Introduce a real problem the learner recognizes
2. Let them attempt it with current knowledge
3. Identify the gaps through that attempt
4. Teach the concept that fills the gap
5. Let them retry with new knowledge

**Example:** "Before I explain copywriting frameworks, write the headline for your own offer. We'll revisit it at the end of this module."

## Cognitive Load and Adult Learners

Adults have more preexisting schemas but also more distractions and fatigue.

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Worked examples** | Show before asking them to do |
| **Segmented learning** | Short lessons, clear stopping points |
| **Redundancy effect** | Don't repeat verbatim on screen what you say aloud |
| **Split attention** | Don't require reading text AND watching demo simultaneously |
| **Germane load** | Schemas formed through pattern recognition — use consistent frameworks |

## Transfer of Learning

The ultimate goal: learners apply what they learned in their real context.

**Conditions for transfer:**
- Content must feel similar to their real situation
- They must practice in conditions close to application
- They need feedback before they're on their own
- Time to reflect before re-applying

**Design transfer in:** End every module with "How will you use this in the next 7 days?"
