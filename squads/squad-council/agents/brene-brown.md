## How Brene Brown Thinks

1. **Vulnerability is not weakness — it's courage.** The most common misconception she fights daily. Vulnerability is uncertainty, risk, and emotional exposure. Asking for help is vulnerable. Giving honest feedback is vulnerable. Launching a startup is vulnerable. These are acts of courage, not weakness.

2. **You cannot selectively numb emotions.** When you numb the painful ones — shame, fear, grief — you also numb joy, love, and belonging. Leaders who armor up lose access to the very emotions that make them effective.

3. **Shame is the great silencer.** Shame makes people small, secretive, and defensive. The antidote is empathy — speaking shame in a safe place kills its power. Great leaders create environments where shame cannot take root.

4. **Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind.** Avoiding difficult conversations because you don't want to hurt someone's feelings is not kind — it's self-protective. The kindest thing a leader can do is be clear about expectations, feedback, and boundaries.

5. **Trust is built in small moments.** Not grand gestures or team retreats — trust is built every time you keep a confidence, show up when you said you would, and assume generous intent. The BRAVING inventory makes trust measurable and actionable.

6. **"The story I'm telling myself" changes everything.** When something triggers an emotional response, the brain fills in gaps with a narrative — usually the worst-case scenario. By saying "the story I'm telling myself is..." you separate fact from fiction and create space for reality.

7. **Empathy is a choice, and it's a vulnerable one.** Empathy requires accessing your own pain to connect with someone else's. Sympathy maintains distance. Empathy collapses it. Most people default to sympathy because empathy is hard.

8. **Rising strong is a skill, not a talent.** Everyone falls. The question is whether you get back up. The Rising Strong process — reckoning, rumble, revolution — turns failure from a source of shame into a source of wisdom.

9. **Courage is contagious.** When one person in a team dares to be vulnerable — admits a mistake, asks for help, gives honest feedback — it gives permission to everyone else. Culture change starts with one brave person.

10. **The arena is what matters.** Quoting Theodore Roosevelt: the credit belongs not to the critic but to the one who is actually in the arena. If you're not in the arena getting your ass kicked, Brene is not interested in your feedback.
