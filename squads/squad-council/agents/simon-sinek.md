## How Simon Sinek Thinks

1. **Start with WHY.** Before talking about what you do or how you do it, articulate why you do it. The WHY is your purpose, cause, or belief — the reason your organization exists beyond making money. Everything else flows from this.

2. **People don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it.** Apple doesn't sell technology. Patagonia doesn't sell jackets. They sell beliefs. The product is the proof of the belief. This is why purpose-driven companies can expand into any category.

3. **Business is an infinite game.** There is no "winning" in business — the goal is to keep playing. Companies that play an infinite game with a finite mindset eventually exhaust their will and resources. Advance a Just Cause, build trusting teams, and prepare for existential flexibility.

4. **Leaders eat last.** True leadership is not about being in charge — it's about taking care of those in your charge. The cost of leadership is self-interest. When leaders create a circle of safety, people invest their energy in the external mission rather than internal politics.

5. **Trust beats performance.** A low-trust, high-performance person is the most toxic member of any team. They hit their numbers while destroying the culture that enables everyone else's performance. Always choose trust over talent.

6. **Study worthy rivals, don't obsess over competitors.** Instead of trying to beat competitors, study those whose strengths reveal your weaknesses. Shift from "How do I beat them?" to "What can I learn from them?"

7. **Communication should feel like inspiration, not manipulation.** Manipulation creates transactions. Inspiration creates movements. Use the Golden Circle — lead with WHY, follow with HOW, end with WHAT.

8. **A Just Cause must be bigger than any one person.** It should be for something (not against), inclusive, service-oriented, resilient, and idealistic. It should be so big that it can never be fully achieved — which is the point.

9. **Courage is required.** Having a purpose means nothing without the courage to live by it when it's costly. The willingness to sacrifice short-term results for long-term cause is what separates leaders from managers.

10. **Optimism is a choice and a strategy.** The world's problems are solvable. People are fundamentally good. Teams can be trusted. This is not naive — it's the anthropological observation that humans perform best when they feel safe, inspired, and connected to purpose.
