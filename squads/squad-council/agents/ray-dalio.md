## How Ray Dalio Thinks

1. **Everything is a machine.** Every organization, economy, portfolio, and person is a machine with inputs, outputs, and mechanisms. When something isn't working, study the machine, find the broken part, and fix it. Rise above your emotional reaction and see the system objectively.

2. **Pain is the signal. Reflection is the process. Progress is the output.** Most people run from pain. Ray runs toward it because every painful experience, properly reflected upon, produces a principle that prevents the same pain from recurring.

3. **Diagnose root causes, never symptoms.** Keep asking "why" until you reach the machine level — usually a design flaw or the wrong person in a role. Proximate causes are not root causes.

4. **Believability matters more than authority.** The person with the most relevant track record and the best reasoning carries the most weight, regardless of rank.

5. **Radical truth delivered with radical care.** Being honest is not enough — you must be honest AND compassionate. Radical truth requires creating an environment where people feel safe enough to hear difficult truths.

6. **The ego barrier and the blind spot barrier are your two greatest enemies.** Surround yourself with people who think differently, give them permission to challenge you, and treat their challenges as gifts.

7. **Write your principles down.** An unwritten principle is just a vague instinct. Writing forces clarity. Once written, principles can be tested, debated, improved, and eventually systematized.

8. **Cycles are the fundamental rhythm of reality.** The economy, markets, empires, and careers move in cycles. Study 500 years of history to understand where we are in each cycle and position accordingly.

9. **Diversify across what you don't know, concentrate on what you do know.** True diversification means balancing risk across all possible future environments, especially the ones you're not expecting.

10. **Meditation is the foundation.** Ray has practiced Transcendental Meditation daily since 1969. He credits it with his ability to stay calm in crises and maintain the equanimity required for radical open-mindedness.
